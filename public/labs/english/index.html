The proof that they repeat: the same words come back

It isn't only the types that repeat — sometimes the exact words do. “Admonish” appears in the Assistant Audit paper (as an antonym) and in the Junior Engineer paper (as a synonym, = “to warn”). “Credulous” (one who readily believes) shows up in the District Organiser paper and again in the Group B paper. So — how much do they vary? The verdict

Format: almost identical. Expect the same families of questions, usually 4 options, 1 mark each, “choose the most appropriate.” Difficulty: easy-to-medium, with a thin layer of hard vocabulary (words like parvenu, coffle, psephologist, leviathan, sumptuary) used to separate top scorers. Content: only the surface shifts. This is great news: you are not preparing for a moving target. Master the ~20 concepts below plus a standard idiom/vocabulary bank, and you cover the overwhelming majority of whatever the next paper throws at you.

01
Parts of Speech
tests: naming the job a word does in a sentence
Core idea

English has eight word-jobs: noun (name), pronoun (stand-in for a noun), verb (action/being), adjective (describes a noun), adverb (describes a verb/adjective/another adverb), preposition (shows relation: in, on, by), conjunction (joins: and, but, because), and interjection (sudden feeling: Ouch!). The trick: a word's part of speech depends on the job it is doing here, not the word itself.

How to do it
  • Find the underlined word and ask: What is it doing?
  • Naming a thing → noun. Replacing a noun → pronoun. Doing/being → verb.
  • Describing a noun → adjective. Describing how/when/where/how-much → adverb.
  • Joining words/clauses → conjunction. Sitting before a noun to show position/relation → preposition.
Analogy

Think of a word as an actor and the sentence as a film. The same actor can play a hero in one film and a villain in another. “Light” is a noun in turn on the light, a verb in light the lamp, and an adjective in a light bag. You name the role, not the actor.

Example
“She pronounced the word quite correctly.” → Adverb — quite tells us how correctly, so it modifies the adverb “correctly.”
Watch out

The classic trap is “all,” “much,” “after,” “while,” “what.” “The children were all in white” → here all stands for the children, so it's a pronoun. “The after effects” → before a noun, describing it, so adjective. Always test the job in this sentence.

Likely next

Expect the same eight categories but with sneakier words — gerunds (verb+ing acting as a noun), words that flip between preposition and adverb (He came in = adverb vs in the room = preposition). Drill the “shape-shifters.”

02
Tenses & Verb Forms
tests: choosing the verb form that matches the time signal
Core idea

A tense answers two questions: when (past/present/future) and how it sits in time (simple = a fact; continuous = ongoing; perfect = completed-before-now; perfect continuous = ongoing-up-to-now). Sentences leave time signals — small words that tell you which form to pick.

How to do it
  • Hunt the signal word: for / since / lately → perfect or perfect-continuous; now / these days / at the moment → continuous; by [time] → perfect.
  • “for the last hour / for ten years” → has/have been + -ing (still going) or has/have + V3.
  • Special cases: I wish + past (regret now) or past perfect (regret about the past); as if / as though + past (unreal).
Analogy

Tenses are camera modes. Simple is a snapshot (it happened). Continuous is video, mid-action. Perfect is a “before/after” split-screen. You read the caption (the signal word) to know which mode the photographer wanted.

Example
“That man ______ at the bus stop for the last hour.” → has been standing — “for the last hour” = action that started in the past and is still continuing → present perfect continuous.
Watch out

I wish he ____ me → use “had told” (past perfect) for a past regret, never “tells.” And after as if the rats ____ it, use “had nibbled” — the unreal comparison forces a backshift in time.

Likely next

More conditionals (If + past → would; If + past perfect → would have) and subjunctive flavours (“It is essential that he be present”). Memorise the signal-word-to-tense table and you'll out-pace the surface wording.

03
Subject–Verb Agreement
tests: making the verb match its real subject in number
Core idea

A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb. Easy — until examiners hide the subject behind extra words or join nouns in tricky ways.

How to do it
  • Strip out the middle (“of…”, “with…”, “as well as…”) and find the head subject. The doctor with his interns is — subject is “doctor,” singular.
  • Two nouns naming one idea/person → singular: “A good man and a useful citizen has passed away.”
  • “Each / Every / one of” → always singular. Uncountables (advice, news, furniture) → singular.
  • Collective nouns acting as a unit → singular (“The committee has issued its report”).
Analogy

The verb is a mirror; it reflects only the real subject standing in front of it — not the crowd of words passing by. “Honour and glory” feels like two, but it's one reward, so the mirror shows “is.”

Example
“Each of the suspected men ______ arrested.” → was — “each” is singular no matter how plural “men” sounds.
Watch out

Don't let the nearest noun fool you. In “The doctor with his interns ___,” the plural “interns” sits closest to the blank, but the subject is the singular “doctor.”

Likely next

Expect “neither…nor / either…or” (verb agrees with the nearer subject), “a number of” (plural) vs “the number of” (singular), and percentages/fractions. Same rule, fancier subjects.

04
Prepositions & Collocations
tests: the small linking word a phrase “demands”
Core idea

Prepositions (in, on, at, for, with, of, from…) show relationships of place, time and direction. But half the questions aren't about logic at all — they're about collocation: certain verbs/adjectives simply “marry” certain prepositions by convention (abstain from, aspire to, consent to, ashamed of).

How to do it
  • Is it literal place/time? Use logic: at a point, on a surface/day, in an enclosed space/month.
  • Is it a fixed pairing? Recall the partner: beneficial for/to, encroach on, afflicted with, dissent from.
  • Phrasal verbs change meaning with the particle: fall for (be fooled), fall back on (rely on), pull off (succeed at), come into (inherit).
Analogy

Collocations are like dance partners. “Aspire” has danced with “to/after” for centuries; pairing it with “for” feels like stepping on toes. The ear that has read a lot simply hears the wrong partner.

Example
“The Commercial Vehicle Union have abstained ______ service today.” → from — “abstain” always pairs with “from.”
Watch out

Two answers can both sound okay (“kept in check with/through the gun”). When logic is split, fall back on the most idiomatic standard pairing. Also: some verbs (enter, reach, marry) take no preposition — beware blanks that tempt you to add one.

Likely next

More phrasal verbs (grow on, give off, worn out, fell for) and cause/effect pairings. Keep a running list of verb+preposition combos from every passage you read.

05
Active & Passive Voice
tests: switching who is the doer vs who receives the action
Core idea

Active: the subject does the action (The cat drank the milk). Passive: the action is done to the subject (The milk was drunk by the cat). To flip: object becomes subject, add the right form of be + past participle (V3), and the old subject moves to a “by…” phrase (often droppable).

How to do it
  • Identify the object of the active sentence — it becomes the new subject.
  • Keep the tense: “drank” (past) → “was drunk”; “is writing” → “is being written”; “has eaten” → “has been eaten.”
  • Add “by + old subject” if it matters; drop it if it's vague (people, someone).
Analogy

It's the same event filmed from two camera angles. Active points the camera at the doer; passive points it at the thing it happened to. Nothing in the world changed — only who's in focus.

Example
“They laughed at his warnings and objected to all his proposals.” (Passive) → His warnings were laughed at and all his proposals were objected to. Note the preposition stays put.
Watch out

Verbs with prepositions keep them in passive (laughed at, objected to). With “believe / say / report,” two passives exist: “People say that Byron lived…” → “Byron is said to have lived…” or “It is said that Byron lived….”

Likely next

Passive of questions and imperatives (“Open the door” → “Let the door be opened”), and reporting-verb passives. The mechanical rule never changes — only the starting sentence.

06
Direct & Indirect (Reported) Speech
tests: reporting someone's words without quotation marks
Core idea

Direct quotes the exact words: He said, “I am tired.” Indirect retells them: He said that he was tired. Three things shift: the tense usually steps back, the pronouns change to match the new speaker, and time/place words move (now→then, today→that day, here→there).

How to do it
  • Pick the right reporting verb: statement → said/told; question → asked/enquired; command → ordered/told; exclamation → exclaimed; wish → wished.
  • Backshift the tense one step (is→was, will→would, did→had done).
  • For questions, drop the question mark and use statement word-order; yes/no questions take “if/whether.”
Analogy

Direct speech is a photocopy of what was said; indirect speech is you describing the photo to a friend later — so “I”, “now”, and “here” all get translated into the time and place you're standing in now.

Example
She said, “When I was a child, I wasn't afraid of ghosts.” → She said that when she had been a child she hadn't been afraid of ghosts.
Watch out

Exclamations and emotion need a “sorrowfully / with delight / that” wrapper, not just “said.” And universal truths don't backshift (The teacher said the earth is round).

Likely next

Reported requests (“Could you repeat…” → “He requested the interviewer to repeat…”), greetings/farewells (“bade farewell”), and exclamations. Learn the reporting-verb menu and the rest is mechanical.

07
Sentence Transformation
tests: rewriting a sentence into a named form, same meaning
Core idea

You're asked to change the shape while keeping the meaning: turn a statement into a question, a positive into a negative, the highest-degree comparison into a comparative, an exclamation into an assertion, and so on.

How to do it
  • Degrees: Superlative ↔ Comparative ↔ Positive. “the most honest of all” → “more honest than anybody else” → “No one else is so honest as him.”
  • Affirmative ↔ Negative: use opposite + a negative. “It is often difficult” → “It is not always easy.”
  • Assertive ↔ Interrogative: “Everybody likes praise.” → “Who does not like praise?” (rhetorical).
  • Exclamatory ↔ Assertive: “I wish I were a queen.” → “Alas! If I were a queen!”
Analogy

It's translating the same sentence into different “moods,” like rephrasing one message as a polite request, a blunt order, or a casual hint — the information is constant, the packaging changes.

Example
“His father is the most honest of all.” (Comparative) → His father is more honest than anybody else.
Watch out

Meaning must not drift. A rhetorical question (“Who does not like praise?”) must keep the original “everybody” sense — not become a real question. Keep “else / any other” when going from superlative to comparative.

Likely next

“So…that” ↔ “too…to,” “Not only…but also,” and simple↔complex↔compound conversions. Build a flip-card deck of these standard swaps.

08
Idioms & Phrases
tests: the hidden meaning behind a fixed expression
Core idea

An idiom means something different from its literal words. “Feet of clay” has nothing to do with feet — it means a hidden weakness in someone admired. These can't be reasoned out word-by-word; they must be known.

How to do it
  • If you know it, answer instantly and move on (idioms are easy marks).
  • If you don't, look for the image behind it: “flog a dead horse” pictures wasted effort → “to do a thing in vain.”
  • Eliminate options that are too literal (“to paint something red” for “red tapism” is a literal trap; the real meaning is “official delay”).
Analogy

Idioms are inside jokes a whole language shares. You either get the joke or you don't — so the only real preparation is hearing enough of them that they become old friends.

Example
“Red tapism” → official delay (bureaucratic foot-dragging), not anything about the colour red.
Watch out

Every idiom question plants a tempting literal option. “Wet blanket” isn't “damp clothes,” it's a person who spoils others' fun. Train yourself to distrust the literal choice.

Likely next

Idioms used inside a sentence (“The tickets cost ____” → “an arm and a leg”) rather than defined in isolation. Learn each idiom with a sample sentence, not just a gloss.

09
Synonyms
tests: the word closest in meaning to a given word
Core idea

A synonym is a word with (nearly) the same meaning. The test is pure vocabulary — but you can stretch a partial memory using word-roots and the sentence's tone.

How to do it
  • Recall the meaning; match the closest option. Thrive = flourish.
  • If unsure, mine the roots: soporific shares “sopor-” (sleep) → “sleep-inducing.”
  • Use context when the word is underlined in a sentence — the tone (positive/negative) rules out options.
Analogy

Synonyms are different brands of the same product. “Begin” and “commence” are two labels on the same tin — interchangeable on the shelf, with only a slight difference in formality.

Example
“The song had a soporific effect on the child.” → sleep-inducing
Watch out

Beware near-misses that share a flavour but not the core: for perception, “reception” looks similar but the meaning is “understanding.” Match meaning, not spelling.

Likely next

Harder, lower-frequency words and synonyms-in-context. Build vocabulary by roots/prefixes (vid/vis = see, bene = good, mal = bad) so unseen words aren't total strangers.

10
Antonyms
tests: the word most opposite in meaning
Core idea

An antonym is the opposite. The danger here is the “same-family” trap — examiners fill the options with synonyms and one true opposite, so you must reverse the meaning, not echo it.

How to do it
  • State the word's meaning, then deliberately flip it. Admonish = scold → opposite = praise/flatter.
  • Cross out the obvious synonyms (they're decoys placed to catch the hasty).
  • Use prefixes: many opposites carry un-, in-, dis-, de- (visible→invisible).
Analogy

If a synonym question asks “who is on this word's team?”, an antonym question asks “who plays against it?” The decoys are teammates put on the field to confuse you — ignore them and find the rival.

Example
Antonym of Admonish → Flatter — “chide, reprove, rebuke” are all synonyms placed as traps.
Watch out

Three of four options often mean the same as the word. If most options agree with each other, the odd one out is very likely your antonym.

Likely next

Antonym pairs in “word : option/option/option” format and tougher words (extant, corpulent, analogous). Learn words in opposite pairs so each one drags its antonym along.

11
One-Word Substitution
tests: the single precise word for a whole phrase
Core idea

You're given a definition (“a person of humble beginnings who has gained wealth”) and must pick the one word that captures it (parvenu). It rewards a precise vocabulary of “labels for ideas.”

How to do it
  • Read the definition and ask, “What is the technical name for this?”
  • Use roots: “appeals to popular desire” + “-agogue” (leader) → demagogue.
  • Eliminate options that are close but not exact (a tyrant rules cruelly; a demagogue manipulates the crowd's feelings — different ideas).
Analogy

It's the reverse of a dictionary. A dictionary gives a word and asks for the meaning; this gives the meaning and asks for the word — like naming the file when you already know what's inside it.

Example
“A political leader appealing to popular desires and prejudices.” → Demagogue
Watch out

Options are often siblings (Tyrant / Demagogue / Populist / Dictator). Pin down the exact nuance in the definition — here, the appeal to emotion is what makes it “demagogue.”

Likely next

“Phobias and -cides,” government/people words, and rarer terms (sumptuary, coffle). Keep a themed list (people, fear-of, killing-of, study-of) and revise weekly.

12
Spelling — spot the misspelt word
tests: recognising the incorrectly spelled option
Core idea

Four words are shown; one is spelled wrong. You're being tested on whether the correct shape of a word is burned into your visual memory.

How to do it
  • Read each word slowly, syllable by syllable, and let your eye flag the one that “looks off.”
  • Check the usual battlegrounds: double letters (occurred), -ent vs -ant (insubordinate), silent letters, -cede/-ceed.
  • If two look fine and two look odd, sound them out against words you know are spelled that way.
Analogy

It's a “spot the difference” puzzle, but the difference is one wrong letter. The more correct spellings you've seen, the faster the fake jumps out — like noticing a typo on a sign you pass every day.

Example
Incentivise / Insignificant / Insubordinant / Insufficient → “Insubordinant” is wrong; it should be insubordinate.
Watch out

The wrong option is usually almost right — one swapped or doubled letter (Prosecuet for prosecute, Leviathane for leviathan). Don't skim; verify the ending.

Likely next

More commonly-confused endings and borrowed words (croissant, leprechaun). Keep a personal “words I always misspell” list — those are exactly what gets tested.

13
Jumbled Sentences (Para-jumbles)
tests: ordering shuffled sentences into a logical flow
Core idea

Four labelled sentences (A–D) are out of order; you rebuild the paragraph. The logic of cause → effect and general → specific tells you the sequence, and the answer options narrow it fast.

How to do it
  • Find the opener — the sentence that introduces the topic and needs no earlier context.
  • Track connectors: therefore, this, so, then, but point backward to what came before.
  • Match pronouns to their nouns (a “this/it” must follow the thing it refers to), then verify against the option list.
Analogy

It's reassembling a comic strip whose panels got shuffled. You look for the panel that sets the scene, then follow the visual “and then… because of that…” links until the story makes sense.

Example
A: Therefore I bought a house… · B: This made me eager to welcome a guest… · C: I intended to be a model citizen… · D: I decided it was time to settle down. → D-C-A-B (decision → intention → action → result).
Watch out

A sentence beginning with “Therefore / This / So” can almost never be first. Use that to eliminate options before you even read carefully.

Likely next

Longer 5–6 sentence jumbles and “find the opening/closing sentence” variants. Practise spotting the topic sentence quickly — it's the master key.

14
Spotting Grammatical Errors
tests: finding the faulty segment (or the error-free sentence)
Core idea

A sentence is split into parts; one part hides a mistake — or you must pick the one fully-correct sentence from four. You're hunting for broken grammar: wrong preposition, tense, article, or agreement.

How to do it
  • Read the whole sentence first for the “off” feeling, then zoom into each segment.
  • Run a checklist: tense consistent? subject–verb match? right preposition? articles (a/an/the) correct? comparison logical?
  • “Since” vs “from”: “Where have you been from such a long time?” should be “for/since.”
Analogy

You're a proofreader scanning a line for the single typo. You don't rewrite the sentence — you just point to the one brick that's out of place in the wall.

Example
“The doctor with his interns / are due / to arrive here by noon.” → error in “are due” — the subject “doctor” is singular, so it must be “is due.”
Watch out

Errors love to hide near distracting plurals and long phrases. In the “error-free sentence” version, articles are the usual trap (“Sugar is sold by the kilogram,” not “by a kilogram”).

Likely next

“Sentence improvement” (choose the best replacement for an underlined part) — the same skill, just asked to also fix the error, not only spot it.

15
Confusing Word Pairs & Word Choice
tests: picking the exact word among look-alikes / sound-alikes
Core idea

Some words look or sound alike but mean different things: affect (to influence) vs effect (a result); continual (repeated) vs continuous (unbroken); captivating (charming) vs captive (imprisoned). The sentence's sense decides which one fits.

How to do it
  • Know the meaning of each look-alike, not just one.
  • Slot each candidate into the sentence and test the sense: “I have three ____ attacks of typhoid” needs “continual” (separate, repeated bouts), not “continuous.”
  • Watch the part of speech the blank needs (affect = verb, effect = noun).
Analogy

These are identical-looking twins with different personalities. If you only know one twin's name, you'll greet the wrong one at the door. Learn both faces.

Example
“The dampness began to ______ the old man's health.” → affected — a verb meaning “to influence”; “effect” would be a noun and wrong here.
Watch out

Sumptuous vs scrumptious, scalded vs scorched, evoke vs provoke — the wrong twin always sounds plausible. Decide by the precise nuance the sentence asks for.

Likely next

More “complete the sentence” items built on near-synonym pairs (cursory/curious, imperious/intimate). Keep a confusables list with one-line distinctions.

16
Sentence Types (Function & Polarity)
tests: naming a sentence by its purpose / by positive vs negative
Core idea

By function, every sentence is one of: assertive (states a fact), interrogative (asks), imperative (commands/requests), exclamatory (strong feeling), or optative (a wish/prayer). Separately, by polarity, it is affirmative (positive) or negative.

How to do it
  • Look at the end mark and the opener. A full stop + plain statement → assertive. A question mark → interrogative.
  • Starts with a verb / gives an instruction → imperative (“Drive to the roundabout”).
  • “What a…! / How…! / Alas!” → exclamatory. “May you…! / Wish…” → optative.
  • Has “not / no / never” → negative; otherwise affirmative.
Analogy

Function is the sentence's job title (it asks, it orders, it cheers). Polarity is just whether it's wearing a “yes” badge or a “no” badge. A sentence can hold a job title and a badge at once.

Example
“Drive to the roundabout and then turn left.” → Imperative (it gives a command).
Watch out

“Do not condemn the judgment of others…” is a negative command — by function it's imperative, by polarity it's negative. If the options mix both kinds, read whether the question wants the purpose or the positivity. (See the flagged item in the keys.)

Likely next

Sentences that blur lines (“Won't you sit down?” = a request dressed as a question) and optative forms. Decide by the sentence's intent, not just its punctuation.

17
Sentence Structure (Simple / Compound / Complex)
tests: counting and classifying the clauses
Core idea

It all comes down to clauses (a clause = subject + verb). Simple = one independent clause. Compound = two independent clauses joined by and/but/or or a semicolon. Complex = one independent + at least one dependent clause (joined by when, because, if, that, which…).

How to do it
  • Count the subject–verb pairs. One pair → simple.
  • Two complete ideas that could each stand alone, glued by and/but/or/; → compound.
  • A “leaning” idea that can't stand alone (starts with when/if/because/that/which) → complex.
Analogy

Think of train cars. Simple = one engine alone. Compound = two engines coupled, each able to run by itself. Complex = one engine pulling a carriage that can't move on its own.

Example
“The moon is full; the stars are out.” → Compound — two independent clauses joined by a semicolon.
Watch out

“Barbara and Joana whispered and giggled all night” looks like two ideas but it's simple — one clause with a compound subject and compound verb (no second subject–verb pair). Count clauses, not just “ands.”

Likely next

Compound-complex sentences and conversions between the three. The clause-counting method handles them all.

18
Clauses (Noun / Adjective / Adverb)
tests: naming the job a dependent clause does
Core idea

A dependent clause does the same job a single word might. A noun clause works as a noun (subject/object). An adjective clause describes a noun (usually starts with who/which/that). An adverb clause tells when/why/how/if (starts with when/because/if).

How to do it
  • Ask what the clause is doing. Could you replace it with “it/this” (a noun)? → noun clause.
  • Does it describe a noun right before it? → adjective clause.
  • Does it answer when/why/where/under-what-condition for the verb? → adverb clause.
Analogy

Same idea as Parts of Speech, scaled up: instead of one word doing a job, a whole little phrase-with-a-verb does it. You still just ask, “What role is this playing in the sentence?”

Example
“Fruit that is grown organically is expensive.” → Adjective clause — it describes “fruit.”
Watch out

“That” starts both noun and adjective clauses. “That he is a cheat is a fact” is a noun clause (it's the subject); “the man that I met” is adjective. Test the job, not the joining word.

Likely next

Spotting clause type inside longer sentences and distinguishing clause vs phrase (a phrase has no subject–verb). Keep the “what job?” question ready.

19
Reading Comprehension (MCQ)
tests: finding answers inside a passage
Core idea

You read a short passage and answer questions whose answers are in the text — directly stated or lightly inferred. The skill is locating evidence, not recalling outside knowledge.

How to do it
  • Skim the passage once for the gist, then read each question.
  • Go back to the passage and underline the exact line that supports your answer — never answer from memory or opinion.
  • For “meaning of word” questions, read the sentence around the word and match the option that fits that context.
Analogy

It's a treasure hunt where the map (the passage) already marks the spot. Your job isn't to guess where the treasure is — it's to walk back and point at the X.

Example
A passage says clean habits add “11 years to a man, seven to a woman,” but the options offer 11+5 and 15+7. → None of these — the text's exact figures (11 and 7) aren't among the choices, so don't force a near-match.
Watch out

Options that are “true in real life” but not stated in this passage are traps. Also watch numbers and small qualifiers (“not,” “only,” “seven”) — they decide between near-identical choices.

Likely next

Inference and tone/“main idea” questions, plus vocabulary-in-context. Always anchor the answer to a specific line; that habit beats every trap.

20
Odd One Out
tests: spotting the word that doesn't share the group's theme
Core idea

Three of the four words share a hidden category (meaning, type, or grammar); one breaks the pattern. Your job is to discover the rule the majority follows.

How to do it
  • Define each word, then look for the trait shared by three of them.
  • Common rules: synonyms vs an antonym, or “same kind of thing” vs an outsider (three pieces of furniture + one tool).
  • The one that won't fit the shared rule is your answer.
Analogy

It's “which of these does not belong?” from a children's puzzle, grown up. Apple, banana, mango, and… potato — three fruits and one vegetable. Find the category, then find the gate-crasher.

Example
Credenza / Tabouret / Chiffonier / Scalpel → Scalpel — the first three are furniture; a scalpel is a surgical tool.
Watch out

The rule can be subtle (Haughty/Reticent/Aloof are “distant/reserved” traits; Sociable is the opposite — so it's odd). Test for meaning-groups, not just spelling or length.

Likely next

Tougher vocabulary sets and grammar-based groups (three adverbs + one adjective). Build category awareness; that's the whole game.

Section 6

Your Prep Plan

You now know the target isn't moving. So the plan is simple: lock down the things that repeat, in the order that earns marks fastest. Don't study everything equally — study the high-frequency items hard and the rare ones lightly.

The 80/20 of this exam

Memorise three things and you bank the easy marks: idioms & phrases, one-word substitutions, and a standard competitive wordlist (synonyms/antonyms). Master six rule-sets and you cover almost all the grammar: tense signal-words, subject–verb agreement, prepositions & collocations, active/passive voice, reported (direct/indirect) speech, and degrees of comparison. Everything else in the paper is a thin top layer.

A 4-week routine

  • Week 1 — Vocabulary core. 15 words/day from the Bank plus a standard SSC/Bank wordlist; always revise yesterday's batch before adding new words.
  • Week 2 — Idioms & one-word subs. 10 + 10 per day, and write one sentence of your own for each so the meaning sticks.
  • Week 3 — Grammar rule-sets. One topic per day using the concept cards in Section 3 — tenses, agreement, prepositions, voice, speech, comparison, error-spotting.
  • Week 4 — Full practice. One past-paper MCQ section daily, timed; then review every wrong answer against the card that explains it.

Daily 30-minute loop

  • 5 min — revise yesterday's words/idioms (spaced repetition).
  • 10 min — learn the new batch for today.
  • 10 min — do 15–20 mixed MCQs.
  • 5 min — log each mistake with the rule behind it, not just the right letter.

In the exam hall

  • Do the vocabulary and idiom items first — they're fast and high-certainty. Build a lead, then slow down.
  • For a grammar blank, find the signal word (a time word, a subject, a preposition trigger) before you even read the options.
  • For error-spotting, test each underlined part against one rule at a time.
  • For odd-one-out, define all four words, then name the category the other three share.
  • Don't over-think “kind of sentence” items — match purpose to label and move on.

What the next paper will likely ask

  • The same topics with fresher, harder words (parvenu, psephologist level) — so keep widening the wordlist.
  • New idioms pulled from the same standard bank you're already learning.
  • Conditionals and the subjunctive layered onto tenses (“It is essential that he be present”).
  • More phrasal verbs and confusable pairs.
  • A reading-comprehension MCQ set, and at least one “none of these” trap.

Bottom line: prioritise the recurring topics. The papers tell you exactly what they value by asking it again and again — you're studying a pattern, not chasing a moving target.

You don't have to memorise a thousand questions — you have to recognise twenty patterns. Learn the pattern once, and every future paper starts to feel familiar.
